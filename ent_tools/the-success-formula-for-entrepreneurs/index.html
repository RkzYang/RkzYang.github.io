the Formula

\(y = f(x) + \epsilon\)

\(y\): success level (the dependent variable): something you can keep in mind but NOT under your control. It’s the outcome that “depends” on something else.

\(x\): the input (the independent variable): something you can control.

\(f\): your method (the functional form): something that determines how your input converts into your outcome.

\(\epsilon\): noise (the error term): good luck and bad luck that are totally inevitable and completely out of your control, but super important so that you should take notice and be mindful of.

How to Use the Formula

setting goal

An entrepreneur should first model his/her \(y\) (success level). Set a goal but then shift the focus to \(f(x)\). By making the shift, it is important to be emotionally detached from \(y\) when working on \(f(x)\). This is because if you are so emotionally fixated on \(y\) that you ignore \(f(x)\), your formula will become \(y = \epsilon\), which means you outcome will totally depend on luck. In this case, you will learn nothing and control nothing. Be fixed on your \(y\) but be flexible on your \(f\), because \(y\) (what you will achieve) depend on \(f(x)\) (your method applied to your input), but not the other way around.

shifting focus

\(f(x)\) is the working process of how you use a given “method” \(f\) to transform your input \(x\), e.g., how you structure your schedule, how you organize your resources, how you utilize your skills, and, in the most general term, your “way of thinking.” When you understand this part, you will never mess up your goal \(y\) with your method \(f\) and your input \(x\). Setting a challenging goal sometimes helps you search for better \(f\). But if you are feeling pressures from \(y\), then get your focus back on \(f(x)\). \(f(x)\) is under your control “right here and right now,” \(y\) is not. You won’t be successful be focusing on success. Do the right things, success will follow.

Develop a skill of shifting focus:

  1. When tasking \(x\), you should throw \(y\) completely into the back of your mind.
  2. When taking rest, revisit your \(y\) and \(\epsilon\) to improve your \(f\).

developing advantage

Best part, your outcome depend on how you deal with \(\epsilon\), flukes or failures. You need to do two things. First, remember \(\epsilon\) is not the whole part of \(y\). Otherwise, you will give up easily after encountering failures. The secret behind most of success is that “winners never quit.” Second, learn from it. Understand that different people have different versions of the formula. Your \(\epsilon\) can also be other people’s \(\epsilon\). But if you can carve out a portion of \(\epsilon\) and put it into your \(f(x)\), which is some trick you figure out before others, or something you know but others don’t, you will have an unfair advantage. In this case, the \(\epsilon\) turns around to help you, by setting up a barrier to prevent others from stealing your trick. How to do it? Simple. Analyze \(\epsilon\) carefully, and treat it as the “feedback” on your new \(f\) (method) and different combinations of \(x\) (input), you will develop a refined \(f(x)\) with private knowledge to your advantage. Now, your \(f(x)\) (method on input) is others’ \(\epsilon\) (luck).

\(\epsilon\) has another nice name – error. Most successful people crack the code from trials and errors. Learning from feedback is key. The progress is a function of the total number of iterations. A greater number of iterations are more likely to generate more helpful data for us to learn. But sometimes the feedback can be quite subtle. Without smoking gun evidence on whether your method will work, we have to rely on our own judgment. When you feel it’s too hard, it might be worth holding on to your old way, or it might be a good time to change your method. But don’t quit too soon, because the path to success is typically non-linear so it is critical to holding on until we hit the “critical mass.”

plug your life into the formula

\(y_{t} = f(x_{t-1}) + \epsilon\)

\(\text{your present outcome = your old method applied to your old input + luck}\)

\(y_{t+1} = f'(x_t) + \epsilon\)

\(\text{your future outcome = your new method applied to your new input + luck}\)

Only by taking full responsibility of your present outcome \(y_{t}\) and setting new goal \(y_{t+1}\) can you feel a desire for new knowledge to create a new method \(f'(x)\). Take control of your new method \(f'(x)\), by preparing yourself to learn and master \(f'\). Then take actions, and most importantly, hold on to process in \(x_t\) that will guarantee success \(y_{t+1}\).
